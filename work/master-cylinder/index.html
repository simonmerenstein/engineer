Hydraulic Brake Master Cylinder

A custom 0.5 in bore master cylinder for the CWRU Baja braking system, designed to increase hydraulic advantage, reduce cost from $470 to $37.80, lower weight from 288 grams to 195 grams per master cylinder, and create a compact alternative to purchased commercial units.

Design Cycle

The project moved through research, seal sizing, CAD, FEA, drawings, CAM, machining, assembly checks, part level, and vehicle level testing.

Research

Bore, travel, and seal architecture

Selected a 0.5 in bore to improve hydraulic advantage while preserving fluid displacement with additional piston travel.

Design

CAD and seal groove dimensions

Modeled the body, cap, piston, spring, end connections, U-cup seal, and O-ring grooves with tight tolerance requirements.

Analysis

Pressure and force validation

Analyzed the 6061-T6 body and cap and 4140 piston using the 1125 lbf piston force and high-pressure worst-case load in Solidworks FEA.

Build

Prototype machining

Manual machining for piston/cap; HSMWorks CAM and Haas VF-3 CNC work with 2 fixtures for the body.

Test

Bench Testing and Vehicle Testing

Bench tested master cylinder to 3000 psi. Ran multiple 4 hour endurance races prior to competition.

FEA Results

The poster analysis checked the 6061-T6 body and 4140 steel piston against brake load cases using pressure in the bore and thread force at the piston.

Results and Lessons

The project produced a complete design package and largely manufactured prototype, plus a clear list of improvements for the next iteration.

  • Reduced the master cylinder cost from $470 for purchased hardware to $37.80 for the in-house design.
  • Reduced weight from 288 grams to 195 grams per master cylinder.
  • FEA confirmed the body and piston exceeded the target factor of safety for the analyzed load cases.
  • The piston and cap were manually machined and hit the tolerances needed for sliding and sealing.
  • The CNC body operation was delayed by machine downtime, but internal features and holes were machined and the assembly test-fit successfully.
  • Prototype drilling used a larger reservoir hole than desired; future iterations should return to the smaller 1/32 in hole to reduce seal wear.
  • Initial assembly revealed a pedal dead zone tied to piston/seal location and port coverage, giving a concrete next design change.
  • Future work should bench test to 1500 psi, evaluate seal life, and refine spring return behavior.
